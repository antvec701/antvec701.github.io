About/CV

I am an economics PhD candidate at the University of Notre Dame specializing in macroeconomics. My primary research focus is the transmission of monetary policy through the banking sector. I maintain general interests in financial markets, fiscal policy, and time series econometrics.

I was born and raised in Bismarck, ND. I studied economics and math as an undergrad at the University of St Thomas. I began my doctoral studies at Indiana University and transferred to Notre Dame after my third year.

Beyond economics, I enjoy playing piano, watching basketball and soccer, graphic design, and hiking. I have a goal of visiting all national parks in the US. You can track my progress by navigating to the "National Parks" tab above.

Research

Working Papers

  • Professional forecasts contain common sentiment components that drive aggregate fluctuations but are not related to existing measures of consumer sentiment. Using Survey of Professional Forecasters data, we remove variation explained by macroeconomic factors and identified structural shocks, then extract factors from the residual forecast component. This approach reveals a substantial factor structure: the first three factors explain roughly a third of residual forecast variation. These factors can be interpreted as shocks that, in a VAR, explain 10 to 15% of medium-horizon aggregate fluctuations in real GDP growth, PCE inflation, the unemployment rate, and the federal funds rate.

  • Note: third-year paper

    During the financial crisis and the pandemic, the Federal Reserve created vast amounts of bank reserves to finance purchases of long-term securities and drive down their yields. I investigate how bank balance sheets react to this reserve creation. I identify a reserve supply shock using agnostic identification of structural vector autoregressive (SVAR) models developed by Arias et al. (2019). Banks react to an unexpected influx of reserves by decreasing loans and increasing holdings of liquid assets - mostly the reserves themselves, but also federally-backed debt securities. I find evidence that banks are actually willing to lend and sell the loans to issuers of asset-backed securities, but they are limited by loan demand.

Publications

  • Note: pre-doctoral publication

    This paper examines the impact of Minneapolis' local minimum wage ordinance on bus ridership. We use a difference-in-differences (DD) strategy to compare changes in ridership at bus stops in Minneapolis near fast-food restaurants to similarly situated stops around the metro area where the minimum wage was unchanged. We find that the minimum wage increase caused bus ridership to increase by 1,828 rides per day or 3.5% of post-treatment daily boardings in Minneapolis. We show that the treatment effect is highly concentrated at bus stops near fast-food restaurants. An increase in the intensive margin of labor, or weekly hours, is consistent with our results and available labor market indicators.

Teaching Philosophy

I often remember the great professors I had as an undergrad that shared their passion for economics. I have an opportunity twice a week to spread this passion to the next generation of students – hopefully some future economists. To me, it does not matter how crazy my week; whenever I am in the classroom, my students are the priority and they deserve as much enthusiasm as I can give. This is not without expecting anything in return. I always challenge my students (e.g. asking them to apply multiple concepts at once), but I also find it important that students find the material attainable. I expect my students to be professional and accountable. I often emphasize skills beyond economics, like basic math skills and writing concisely. However, my primary goal is to make the economic intuition clear. I always motivate course topics by relating them to previous material or current events. In the end, I enjoy my students' thanks not because I need it, but because I know they've learned something valuable.

National Parks

In 2016, I set a goal of traveling to all national parks in the United States. I have been to 30 out of 63 since then. I maintain a ranking of all parks I have visited. Parks are given a score from 1 to 10 in the following weighted categories:

  • Macro-Scenery
    30%
  • Micro-Scenery
    25%
  • Wildlife
    12.5%
  • Uniqueness
    10%
  • Crowd
    7.5%
  • Trails
    7.5%
  • Cleanliness
    5%
  • History
    2.5%

As an economist, I divide scenery into macro- and micro-scenery! Macro-scenery is large scale viewpoints while micro-scenery encompasses rock features, trees, waterfalls, etc. Of course, rankings are subjective. However, I hope that the categories add some objectivity by allowing easy comparison of parks along specific metrics. The category system rewards well-rounded parks that feature all the strengths of a great national park. You can see all the ratings details and personalize the rankings by downloading the worksheet below. All pictures are my own.

Rankings

30. Gateway Arch (MO)

Best categories: uniqueness, cleanliness, history

One of these parks is not like the others, and it's this one. Many people are shocked to find out that the Gateway Arch is a national park. After all, it is urban, small, and has some squirrels for wildlife. However, the Gateway Arch is still an impressive monument and architectural feat. The grounds are also well kept. I'm still wondering how they keep the grass that lush.

29. Pinnacles (CA)

Best categories: micro-scenery, crowd, cleanliness

America's most perplexing national park, and the only one that urges you to bring a flashlight? The park's main attraction are its talus caves, which are caves formed from the openings between giant boulders. I'm sure rock climbers love the park for a little more than those!

28. Great Sand Dunes (CO)

Best categories: macro-scenery, uniqueness, cleanliness

If I were more talented, I would go sandboarding. Otherwise, there is not much else to this park. Don't get me wrong though; it's pretty crazy that there are sand dunes among the mountains of Colorado. It makes for one spectacular view.

27. Indiana Dunes (IN)

Best categories: micro-scenery, trails, crowd

The National Park Service v urban sprawl. Chicago on one side and a steel mill on the other. In my opinion, this park is more about preservation than exploration. The park is intermingled with highways and factories. Despite this, it is surprisingly easy to find solitude on one of the park's many peaceful trails. Otherwise, hit the beach like all the locals do.

26. Mammoth Cave (KY)

Best categories: uniqueness, crowd, history

A cool park that's worth a visit if you're in the area. However, it lacks something spectacular that would make it stand out. That is, besides the title of world's longest known cave system! This cave really is huge.

25. Petrified Forest (AZ)

Best categories: uniqueness, crowd, cleanliness

Petrified Forest is one of four national parks with badlands. Being from a state containing one of these parks, I can say that the badlands of Petrified Forest are rather underwhelming. Additionally, the park's namesake doesn't add much value. However, we did meet a very friendly ranger here who gave us our own private reptile presentation (due to lack of other visitors). This is a reminder of how dedicated and helpful many of the rangers are.

24. Voyageurs (MN)

Best categories: wildlife, crowd, cleanliness

Nothing spectacular here, but still worth a visit. Voyageurs' charm is in its peace and quiet. I went in the summer, but its appeal is likely greater in the fall when the leaves turn. Short, family-friendly hikes accessible by car are only available around the Ash River Visitors Center. Anyone with a longer stay should certainly venture out on the water. Boat tours must be booked in advance.

23. Wind Cave (SD)

Best categories: micro-scenery, uniqueness, cleanliness

Wind Cave is probably the least spectacular of the cave parks in beauty. However, it's crazy to think of the cave explorers who still spend days at a time discovering new portions of the cave. It's massive! Also, it is one of the few places in the world where you can see boxwork (the calcite formations seen pictured). Wind Cave is often compared to its more interesting, non-national park neighbor, Jewel Cave.

22. Mesa Verde (CO)

Best categories: uniqueness, cleanliness, history

The park does a good job of preserving native American history and allowing visitors to understand life back then. And the landscape is pretty cool if you aren't from the southwest. I decided not to book a tour. Unfortunately, I was unaware that the only self-guided cliff dwelling was closed. Don't make the same mistake as me!

21. Capitol Reef (UT)

Best categories: micro-scenery, crowd, cleanliness

I'm still trying to figure out what a reef is when it's not underwater, but that doesn't mean there aren't plenty of interesting things in this park. Capitol Reef is more of the typical southern Utah: cool rocks and some arches. For those interest, it is definitely worth a visit.

20. Great Smoky Mountains (NC, TN)

Best categories: micro-scenery, trails, history

The Smoky Mountains are awesome! I went in the early spring expecting highs in the 60s and dead trees. Instead, there was a dusting of snow in the morning and sweeping clouds across the summit all day. It was absolutely gorgeous. Of course, these mountains can't hold their own when compared to others in the west. Solid mid-tier park. Worth a visit if you're nearby.

19. Kings Canyon (CA)

Best categories: micro-scenery, crowd, cleanliness

I admit to cheating on this park a little bit. I have stepped foot in the boundaries of the park, but I didn't get to see much of it. That's because a vast majority of Kings Canyon is a remote wilderness. So, I imagine this is an underrated park, just like its neighbor, Sequoia. However, even a sliver of the drive (which technically is not even within the park) is pretty spectacular.

18. Channel Islands (CA)

Best categories: micro-scenery, wildlife, crowd

It's hard to believe that the water could be this clear so close to LA. Day trips to the smallest island are available from Ventura. The wildlife has got to be the best part – except the seagulls! Inspiration Point (seen pictured) and Arch Rock are also pretty cool. Otherwise, there isn't much else on the island.

17. Hawaii Volcanoes (HI)

Best categories: macro-scenery, micro-scenery, uniqueness

The park contains two of the Big Island's volcanoes: Kilauea and Mauna Loa. The former is one of the world's most active volcanoes. Timing your visit with an eruption can boost the excitement (so long as you obey the park closures). When there is not much activity, you can still see the caldera, lava fields, sulfur vents, lava tubes, and a sea arch.

16. Sequoia (CA)

Best categories: micro-scenery, uniqueness, trails

This park is probably underrated. Of course, it's super famous for the largest tree in the world, General Sherman. But most tourists (including me) never make it to the other half. Those who do are rewarded with the beauty of the southern Sierra Nevada wilderness. For the rest of us, there is still plenty to enjoy. The accessible portion of the park has tons of easy hikes in peaceful forests and meadows. Tunnel Log, Moro Rock, and Crystal Cave (buy tickets in advance) are also popular.

15. Black Canyon (CO)

Best categories: macro-scenery, uniqueness, crowd

This park packs a lot into a small area! You can almost always hear the Gunnison river. But only when you step right up to the edge does the river get really loud. It echoes all the way up the cliffs, at most over 2,700 feet in height. The Gunnison cut straight down, allowing visitors to see rock layers stacked vertically and horizontally. If you have a vehicle that goes into low gear, be sure to go to the bottom of the less steep portion of the canyon via East Portal Road. The bottom is incredibly quiet and peaceful. So, keep it secret!

14. Bryce Canyon (UT)

Best categories: macro-scenery, micro-scenery, uniqueness

The second of Utah's "Mighty Five" on this list is just as amazing as the others. It's hard to think of another park in the southwest as unique as this one. The orange spires – called Hoodoos – are pretty cool from above and below. Bryce Canyon's only drawback compared to its Utah peers is that it is pretty small.

13. North Cascades (WA)

Best categories: macro-scenery, micro-scenery, trails

If you're looking for a less crowded, low elevation alternative to Glacier, then North Cascades is it. The mountains are stunning! There are plenty of places to explore, but only a single road cuts through the massive park. So, strenuous hikes are the only way to reach the most beautiful areas. The Washington Pass area (though technically not in the park boundaries) has moderate shorter hikes that are incredible. Seeing the bright turquoise waters at Diablo Lake is also an experience.

12. Badlands (SD)

Best categories: macro-scenery, uniqueness, crowd

Tourists often stop for a quick visit off I-90 on their way to Yellowstone and fail to give Badlands its fair share, maybe because Badlands is barely noticeable from the highway. It isn't until the flat prairie meets the dramatic spires until you are blown away. Badlands offers several magnificient views, each one better than the last, and many easy, short hikes that let you explore in-and-around the buttes. The most impressive part is Badland's relatively easy access to its wilderness. Anyone with the right vehicle should journey along the park's gravel roads where few others travel.

11. Zion (UT)

Best categories: macro-scenery, micro-scenery, trails

Zion is probably the best park to be completely ruined by the crowd. Other parks are busy, but you can often avoid the crowd. This is nearly impossible in Zion because no cars are allowed in the valley. Instead, you are crammed into buses. For me, this made Zion much less enjoyable than it should be. The valley is gorgeous, especially viewed from Angel's Landing. Furthermore, hiking waist-deep in the Virgin River Narrows is a unique experience and a great escape from the heat.

10. Yellowstone (WY, MT, ID)

Best categories: micro-scenery, uniqueness, history

You are probably surprised to see Yellowstone so low on the list, but what makes America's first park so famous is what disappoints. Crowds swarm Old Faithful like clockwork and the Grand Prismatic Spring isn't as colorful as the photos imply. Yet Yellowstone is still an amazing park. To enjoy your visit, either get up early, stay up late, or find a quiet hike to discover the park like it was when it was founded. The Grand Canyon and falls is also well worth it.

9. Theodore Roosevelt (ND)

Best categories: micro-scenery, wildlife, crowd

Perhaps the most underrated national park. While several other parks surpass this one in beauty, Theodore Roosevelt's strength is that it is well-rounded. I have never been in the park without seeing at least bison, prairie dogs, and horses. Also, don't discount the North Dakota badlands. The Painted Canyon overlook is lush, colorful, and seemingly 2D. And the best part: the crowd is never an issue. Anyone with time should visit the more rugged and remote north unit.

8. Grand Canyon (AZ)

Best categories: macro-scenery, trails, history

Yes, it's true; everyone should see the Grand Canyon in their lifetime. I'll leave it there.

7. Glacier (MT)

Best categories: macro-scenery, wildlife, trails

The peaks aren't very high (around 8,000 feet), but what makes Glacier so unique is its access to the near-top via the Going-to-the-Sun Road. This allows anyone to enjoy the majestic, 360-degree view of the park. Visitors are often greeted at the top by friendly wildlife. But as expected, there isn't much room at the top of a mountain. Get there early – EARLY – to get a parking spot. Also, find other beautiful places around the park where the crowd doesn't go, like St. Mary's Lake. I was surprised that there was almost nobody at this postcard-worthy lake.

6. Arches (UT)

Best categories: macro-scenery, micro-scenery, uniqueness

This is the park where I discovered the vertical panorama. See also Sequoia. Arches is more famous than its neighbor, Canyonlands, and for good reason – despite all the amazing things I have to say about the latter. Arches has over 2,000 natural arches! But the fame comes with the crowd. Arches has struggled in recent years to deal with the crowd, but don't let this deter you.

5. Canyonlands (UT)

Best categories: macro-scenery, micro-scenery, crowd

Canyonlands might as well be called the Grand Canyon without the crowd. Canyonlands is so unexpectedly stunning. The colorful, rugged valley below has the looming La Sal Mountains as its backdrop. The canyon even has natural arches of its own. Unfortunately, I didn't get much time here. I saw just one of the park's three sectors, and I can't imagine the beauty of the other two. This park is easily the one I want to revisit the most.

4. Mount Rainier (WA)

Best categories: macro-scenery, micro-scenery, trails

Mount Rainier commands your attention from far outside the park boundaries. It only gets more magnificient the closer you get. Seeing Reflection Lake, Narada Falls, and the Nisqually River is enough, but I highly recommend hiking the Skyline Loop to get the full experience. This is perhaps the best hike I have ever done. On the trail, you can see as far away as Mount Hood in Oregon. And don't forget to turn around. Mount Rainier is still there to remind you of its superiority.

3. Olympic (WA)

Best categories: macro-scenery, micro-scenery, uniqueness

Olympic blends mountains, rainforests, and beaches into one huge park. Hurricane Ridge exceeded my expectations. Sol Duc Falls (seen pictured) is also a highlight. The stroll along Rialto Beach is something I'll be replaying in my head for a while. But, in a park this expansive, there is bound to be something worth skipping. That is the Hall of Mosses. To me, this is a quasi-tourist trap. Spend your time at the other tremendous places in the park.

2. Yosemite (CA)

Best categories: macro-scenery, trails, history

Yosemite simply has everything! The valley is gorgeous from every angle and has an astonishing amount of true icons: El Capitan, Half Dome, Yosemite Falls, Bridalveil Falls, just to name a few. And Tunnel View – perhaps the most famous view in America – presents it all like a menu. I haven't even mentioned Glacier Point. One month in Yosemite is not enough!

1. Grand Teton (WY)

Best categories: macro-scenery, wildlife, trails

Grand Teton is the "token" national park. Yet, it is simply different viewpoints of the same peaks. But that's the point! You could stare at the Tetons for hours. When you leave you'll be looking back. That is all this park needs to take the number one spot. The rest is bonus. Wildlife is plentiful around the park. Grand Teton is where I saw my first bear up close. Don't let that scare you away. Everyone should see the Tetons.

General Park-Visiting Advice

Feel free to email me for park-specific advice!

  • Get up early or stay late to beat the crowd and the heat.

  • If there is a non-loop park road, drive all the way to the end and make stops as you work back towards the entrance to avoid crowds.

  • The rangers love to share cool info if you're willing to hear it.

  • Check well in advance if reservations are required for major parks (e.g. Rocky Mountain, Zion, Yosemite, etc.).

  • If you plan to visit three of more parks with an entrance fee in a year, be sure to buy the annual pass to save money.

  • Park entrance fees are usually free on MLK Day, the first day of National Park Week, Juneteenth, the anniversary of the Great American Outdoors Act, National Public Lands Day, and Veterans Day.
